Ancient Rome was one of the most powerful civilisations in history, lasting from the founding of the city in 753 BC to the fall of the Western Roman Empire in 476 AD — over a thousand years of continuous development.

Rome began as a small city-state on the banks of the Tiber River in central Italy. According to tradition, it was founded by Romulus, who became its first king. The early Roman Kingdom gave way to the Roman Republic around 509 BC, when the Romans expelled their king and established a system of government led by two elected consuls and a Senate.

The Republic expanded aggressively through conquest. Rome fought three major wars against the Carthaginian Empire — the Punic Wars — between 264 and 146 BC. The most famous of these saw the Carthaginian general Hannibal cross the Alps with war elephants to invade Italy. Rome ultimately prevailed and destroyed Carthage, cementing its dominance of the Mediterranean world.

Julius Caesar was a pivotal figure in Rome's transition from Republic to Empire. His military campaigns conquered Gaul (modern France and Belgium), and he later crossed the Rubicon River with his army — an act of civil war — to seize control of Rome. He was assassinated on the Ides of March (March 15) in 44 BC by senators who feared he was becoming a tyrant. His death sparked further civil wars, ultimately leading to his adopted heir Octavian taking power as Rome's first emperor, Augustus Caesar, in 27 BC.

At its height under Emperor Trajan in around 117 AD, the Roman Empire stretched from Britain in the northwest to Mesopotamia in the east, and from the Rhine and Danube rivers in the north to the Sahara Desert in the south. It encompassed roughly 5 million square kilometres and a population of 50 to 90 million people.

Roman engineering achievements were remarkable. They built an extensive road network spanning over 400,000 kilometres, aqueducts that supplied cities with fresh water, and monumental structures including the Colosseum, the Pantheon, and Hadrian's Wall. Roman concrete, known as opus caementicium, was so durable that many structures survive to this day.

The fall of the Western Roman Empire in 476 AD is one of history's most debated events. Factors include military pressure from Germanic tribes, economic troubles, political instability, overextension of the empire, and the gradual weakening of central authority. The Eastern Roman Empire, known as the Byzantine Empire, continued for nearly another thousand years until the fall of Constantinople in 1453.
