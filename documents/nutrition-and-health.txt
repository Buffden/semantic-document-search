Nutrition is the science of how food affects the body. The food we eat provides energy and the raw materials needed to build and repair tissues, regulate body processes, and support immune function. Poor nutrition is one of the leading contributors to chronic disease worldwide.

Macronutrients — carbohydrates, proteins, and fats — are needed in large quantities and provide the body with energy. Carbohydrates are the body's preferred energy source. Complex carbohydrates found in whole grains, legumes, and vegetables are digested slowly and provide steady energy. Simple carbohydrates in sugary drinks and processed foods cause rapid spikes in blood sugar.

Proteins are essential for building and repairing muscle, producing enzymes and hormones, and supporting immune function. Complete proteins, which contain all essential amino acids, are found in animal products like meat, fish, eggs, and dairy. Plant proteins — beans, lentils, tofu, quinoa — can be combined to provide all essential amino acids.

Dietary fats are critical for absorbing fat-soluble vitamins, producing hormones, and protecting organs. Unsaturated fats found in olive oil, nuts, avocados, and fatty fish are associated with reduced risk of heart disease. Saturated fats, found in red meat and full-fat dairy, should be consumed in moderation. Trans fats, once common in processed foods, are now largely banned in many countries due to their strong link to cardiovascular disease.

Micronutrients — vitamins and minerals — are needed in smaller amounts but are essential for health. Vitamin D, often called the sunshine vitamin, is critical for bone health and immune function. Iron carries oxygen in the blood; deficiency causes anaemia. Calcium supports bone density. B vitamins are involved in energy metabolism and nerve function.

Hydration is often overlooked. The body is roughly 60 percent water, and even mild dehydration impairs cognitive function, physical performance, and mood. Most adults need around 2 to 3 litres of water per day depending on activity level and climate.

Dietary patterns matter more than individual foods. The Mediterranean diet — rich in vegetables, fruit, whole grains, fish, and olive oil — is consistently associated with lower rates of heart disease, diabetes, and cognitive decline. Ultra-processed foods, which are high in sugar, salt, and refined fats, are linked to obesity, inflammation, and increased mortality.
